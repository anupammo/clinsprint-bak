Clinical SAS

Clinical Statistical Analysis Software

Statistical Analysis Software (SAS) is a biostatistical tool used to manage and generate tables,listings,graphs,reports for clinical study/trial data.SAS is widely used in clinical trial data analysis in pharmaceutical, biotech and clinical research companies.SAS programmers play an important role in clinical trial data analysis. In addition to doctors and clinicians who collect clinical trial data, the group conducting data analysis includes statisticians, clinical data managers (CDMs) and SAS programmers. Statisticians provide the ideas and methods of the data analysis, clinical data managers manage the collected data and control the data quality. In between, SAS programmers implement the analysis methods on the collected data and provide the study summary tables, data listing and graphs to the statisticians or clinicians to write study report. SAS programmers work closely with statisticians and data managers. They provide the link between raw data and the analysis.

The Pharmaceutical industry was the first vertical market that SAS software truly dominated during the mid ‘70s. Dominate in this case means SAS Institute penetrated 100% of those pharmaceutical companies with IBM mainframes running OS. Several pharma companies used SAS software for clinical trials on timesharing bureaus because they didn’t have IBM mainframes.

Pharma, especially for clinical trials, embraced SAS software because it was a:

  • 1 Programming Language — any data could be read, any report could be generated. A programmer with equal skills in the SAS language and a standard programming language (COBOL, Assembler or FORTRAN) could accomplish these tasks more quickly using SAS software.
  • 2 Data Management System - Once clinical data was input into SAS datasets, even programming novices could sort, merge, match and maintain archives.
  • 3 Proven Statistical Software that not only generated standard (to SAS Institute) reports but could output their results into SAS datasets for use in the SAS programming language to generate whatever specialized reports were required.

None of SAS Institute’s contemporary competitors could claim such wide ranging capability.

In addition to all the above, SAS software had an additional, profound advantage: it recognized and could incorporate missing data in its statistical processing. With other competitors, missing data had to be coded for the statistics to be properly calculated.

For example, in May 4 patient’s weights were recorded as 250, 300, 180 and 200 lbs for an average of 930/4 = 232.5 lbs. In June, the patient’s weights are recorded as 220, 290, blank, and 190. Patient 3 was dropped from the trial. When SAS software reads the data it will read four data points, but patient 3 will be recorded as “.” or missing because the entry is blank. SAS software would automatically calculate the average to be 700/3 = 233.33 lbs. Competing software would read the blank as zero and calculate the average as 700/4 = 175 UNLESS a regimen was established whereby a blank was identified as missing and recoded so that the statistical processes would know how to process them properly.
